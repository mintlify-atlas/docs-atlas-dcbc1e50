Your AI-generated speech is a strong foundation, but adding your personal touch will make it truly shine. This guide shows you how to review, refine, and perfect your speech.

## Initial Review Process

When you first receive your generated speech, approach it methodically:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Read It Through Once">
    Read the entire speech from start to finish without stopping. Get an overall sense of the flow and structure.
    
    **Don't edit yet**—just read and absorb.
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Read It Aloud">
    This is crucial. Reading aloud helps you:
    - Catch awkward phrasing
    - Identify tongue-twisters
    - Feel the natural rhythm
    - Notice pacing issues
    
    <Tip>
    Record yourself reading it. You'll hear things you miss when reading silently.
    </Tip>
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Check Against Your Goals">
    Ask yourself:
    - Are all my key points included?
    - Is the tone appropriate?
    - Is the length right for my time slot?
    - Does it match the occasion?
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Note Your Reactions">
    As you review, mark:
    - ✓ Parts you love
    - ? Parts you're unsure about
    - ✗ Parts that need changes
  </Step>
</Steps>

## What to Look For

### Content Accuracy

<Warning>
Always verify that facts, names, dates, and figures are correct. The AI generates based on your input, but you're responsible for accuracy.
</Warning>

**Check these elements:**

- Names (people, companies, places)
- Dates and timelines
- Numbers and statistics
- Job titles and roles
- Relationships between people
- Specific events or occasions

### Flow and Structure

A well-structured speech has:

1. **Strong Opening** - Grabs attention and sets the tone
2. **Clear Body** - Develops your key points logically
3. **Smooth Transitions** - Connects ideas naturally
4. **Memorable Closing** - Leaves a lasting impression

<Accordion title="Common Flow Issues and Fixes">
  **Issue:** Abrupt transitions between points
  
  **Fix:** Add connecting phrases like:
  - "This reminds me of..."
  - "Speaking of which..."
  - "Building on that idea..."
  - "Another example of this is..."
  
  ---
  
  **Issue:** Too much time on one point
  
  **Fix:** Condense or split the section. Each key point should get roughly equal attention unless one is deliberately your focus.
  
  ---
  
  **Issue:** Weak ending
  
  **Fix:** End with a call to action, heartfelt wish, or memorable statement that reinforces your main message.
</Accordion>

### Tone Consistency

Your speech should maintain a consistent tone throughout, unless you deliberately shift it.

**Red flags:**
- Formal language mixed with slang
- Serious topic treated too lightly
- Funny opening but somber middle
- Professional content with overly casual delivery

<Note>
Some tone shifts are intentional and effective (starting light, ending serious). The key is that shifts should feel purposeful, not accidental.
</Note>

## Making Adjustments

### Minor Edits

For small tweaks, you can edit the generated text directly:

**Word Substitutions**
- Replace generic words with specific ones
- Change formal words to casual (or vice versa)
- Fix awkward phrasing

**Example:**
- Generated: "John is a good friend"
- Edited: "John is my most loyal friend"

**Adding Personal Touches**
- Insert specific names
- Add inside jokes (if appropriate)
- Include recent updates
- Reference shared experiences

<Tip>
Look for places where you can replace "someone" or "something" with actual names and specifics.
</Tip>

### Major Revisions

If larger changes are needed, consider these approaches:

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Regenerate with Better Inputs">
    **When to use:** The overall structure or tone is off
    
    **How to do it:**
    1. Note what's wrong with the current version
    2. Adjust your key points to be more specific
    3. Change the tone selection if needed
    4. Generate a fresh version
    
    This often works better than heavy editing.
  </Tab>
  
  <Tab title="Keep the Structure, Rewrite Sections">
    **When to use:** You like the flow but some sections need work
    
    **How to do it:**
    1. Keep the opening and closing
    2. Rewrite the middle sections that aren't working
    3. Ensure transitions still flow smoothly
  </Tab>
  
  <Tab title="Merge Multiple Versions">
    **When to use:** You want the best of several attempts
    
    **How to do it:**
    1. Generate 2-3 versions
    2. Take the best opening from version A
    3. Use middle sections from version B
    4. Grab the closing from version C
    5. Smooth the transitions between merged sections
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Iterating on Results

Rarely is the first version perfect. Here's how to iterate effectively:

### The Three-Pass Method

<Steps>
  <Step title="Pass 1: Content">
    Focus only on content:
    - Are all facts correct?
    - Are key points complete?
    - Is anything missing?
    - Is anything unnecessary?
    
    Make content changes first, before polishing language.
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Pass 2: Language">
    Focus on how things are said:
    - Is the language clear?
    - Are sentences varied in length?
    - Does it sound natural?
    - Are there better word choices?
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Pass 3: Delivery">
    Focus on how you'll present it:
    - Where will you pause?
    - What will you emphasize?
    - Is it easy to read from?
    - Are there pronunciation challenges?
  </Step>
</Steps>

### Test and Refine

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Read to a Friend" icon="user-group">
    Get feedback from someone you trust. They'll catch things you miss and tell you if it resonates.
  </Card>
  
  <Card title="Time Yourself" icon="clock">
    Speak at your natural pace and time it. Adjust length as needed. Remember: most people speak 125-150 words per minute.
  </Card>
  
  <Card title="Record and Listen" icon="microphone">
    Hearing yourself helps identify pacing issues, unclear sections, and places where you stumble.
  </Card>
  
  <Card title="Take a Break" icon="coffee">
    Step away for an hour or a day, then review with fresh eyes. You'll notice new opportunities for improvement.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Polishing Your Speech

### Strengthening Your Opening

The first 10-30 seconds are critical. Your opening should:

**Option 1: Start with a Story**
> "Five years ago, Sarah called me at 2 AM. She wasn't in trouble—she'd just met the person she was going to marry..."

**Option 2: Start with a Question**
> "When was the last time you felt truly inspired by your work?"

**Option 3: Start with a Bold Statement**
> "Today marks the biggest pivot in our company's 20-year history."

**Option 4: Start with Gratitude**
> "Standing here today, looking at all of you, I'm overwhelmed by gratitude..."

<Warning>
Avoid weak openings like "Hi everyone, thanks for coming" or "I'm not good at speeches, but..." Start strong!
</Warning>

### Enhancing Your Closing

Your closing should be memorable and purposeful:

**Techniques for strong closings:**

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Circle Back to Your Opening">
    Reference something from your opening to create a satisfying sense of completion.
    
    **Example:**
    - Opening: "When John first joined our team, he had one simple question: 'How can I help?'"
    - Closing: "And five years later, he's still asking that same question—except now, he's helping hundreds of people every day."
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="End with a Call to Action">
    Give your audience something specific to do or think about.
    
    **Examples:**
    - "So I challenge each of you: find one way to innovate this week."
    - "Let's raise our glasses to the happy couple!"
    - "Now let's go out there and make this vision a reality."
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Leave Them with a Powerful Quote or Statement">
    End with words that will stick in their minds.
    
    **Examples:**
    - "As Maya Angelou said, 'People will forget what you said, but they will never forget how you made them feel.'"
    - "The future isn't something we wait for—it's something we create, together, starting today."
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Express Heartfelt Emotion">
    For personal speeches, genuine emotion creates lasting impact.
    
    **Examples:**
    - "I couldn't be more proud to call you my friend."
    - "Thank you for 20 incredible years. Here's to the next chapter."
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### Adding Rhetorical Power

Elevate your speech with these techniques:

**Rule of Three**
> "We've grown stronger, smarter, and more united."

**Repetition for Emphasis**
> "We didn't give up when times were tough. We didn't give up when critics doubted us. And we won't give up now."

**Contrast**
> "Three years ago, we were struggling to serve 100 customers. Today, we serve 10,000."

**Questions (Rhetorical)**
> "What does success look like? It looks like this room, filled with people who refused to quit."

<Tip>
Don't overuse these techniques. One or two powerful moments are better than constant rhetorical flourishes.
</Tip>

## Final Preparation

### Format for Delivery

Make your speech easy to deliver:

**Print Formatting Tips:**
- Use large font (14-16pt)
- Double-space lines
- Bold key words or phrases
- Add pauses with "/" or "..."
- Number pages clearly
- Print on one side only

**Digital Formatting Tips:**
- Use a teleprompter app if available
- Increase screen brightness
- Use dark mode with white text (easier to read)
- Have a backup copy on another device

### Practice, Practice, Practice

<Steps>
  <Step title="Practice 5-7 Times">
    Read through your speech multiple times to internalize it. You don't need to memorize, but you should be very familiar with the content.
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Practice with Distractions">
    Simulate the real environment. Practice with background noise, standing up, or with people around.
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Practice Recovery">
    Intentionally skip sections or lose your place, then practice getting back on track smoothly.
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Do a Full Dress Rehearsal">
    Practice in similar conditions to the actual event: same outfit, same time of day, same posture.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
Practice in front of a mirror or record yourself. Watch for distracting habits like fidgeting, excessive hand movements, or lack of eye contact.
</Tip>

### Final Checklist

Before your speech, verify:

- [ ] All names are spelled and pronounced correctly
- [ ] All facts and figures are accurate
- [ ] Speech timing matches your allotted slot
- [ ] You have a printed or backed-up copy
- [ ] You've practiced at least 5 times
- [ ] You know your opening and closing by heart
- [ ] You've marked where to pause or emphasize
- [ ] You've removed any tongue-twisters
- [ ] The tone matches the occasion
- [ ] You're comfortable with every word

## Troubleshooting Common Issues

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="My speech feels too generic">
    **Solution:** Add more personal details and specific examples. Replace general statements with concrete stories and data.
    
    - Generic: "She's a great leader"
    - Specific: "When the project was failing, she worked alongside us for 72 hours straight, ordered pizza at midnight, and never complained once"
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="It's too long / too short">
    **Solution for too long:** 
    - Remove less important key points
    - Condense similar points
    - Cut redundant examples
    
    **Solution for too short:**
    - Add an anecdote or story
    - Expand on your most important point
    - Include a relevant quote
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="It doesn't sound like me">
    **Solution:** Read it aloud and replace words you wouldn't naturally use. If you'd never say "moreover," change it to "also" or "plus."
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="The tone feels off">
    **Solution:** Regenerate with a different tone setting, or manually adjust:
    - Too formal? Replace complex words with simpler ones
    - Too casual? Add structure and remove slang
    - Too serious? Add a light moment or humor
    - Too jokey? Add sincerity and depth
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="I keep stumbling over certain parts">
    **Solution:** Those sections need rewriting. If you can't say it smoothly after 3-4 tries, change the words. Simple, clear language is always better than complex phrasing.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## When to Stop Editing

Know when your speech is ready:

<Note>
Your speech is ready when:
- You can read it smoothly without stumbling
- It fits your time slot (with 30 seconds of buffer)
- All facts are accurate
- It sounds authentic to your voice
- You feel confident delivering it
- You've practiced it enough to be comfortable

**Perfection isn't the goal**—connection is. A slightly imperfect speech delivered with confidence beats an over-polished script read nervously.
</Note>
